Scenario 2

Juvenile Probation, Prosecutor's Office and School

START

Fifteen year-old James was taken into custody by a School Resource Officer (SRO) for failing to follow school rules or policies and being "ungovernable," defined in the state's children's code as a violation of law.

BACK NEXT

The SRO brought James to a Multi-Agency Resource Center where the staff completed an intake form, which did not include any information about James' school attendance or James's past school disciplinary history.

BACK NEXT

James gets a court date to contest his governability, before which the prosecuting attorney's office and the probation office both contact the school.

The prosecuting attorney's office and the probation office want James's school attendance record, past school disciplinary history, and information about services and interventions the school has offered James in the past.

BACK NEXT

It is not clear what the prosecuting attorney and probation office want to do with the information.

These offices may use the information obtained to prosecute James for being ungovernable as alleged in the petition.

The offices may also use the information to divert James from adjudication and into some type of alternative program.

What does the law permit the school to do with the information?

  • The school may share the educational records with prior parental consent;
  • The school may share the educational records if the prosecutor/probation office serve the school with a court order or subpoena as long as "reasonable efforts" were made to notify the student's parent of the order or subpoena before service and the school made a "reasonable effort" to notify the student's parent of the order or subpoena before complying with it;
  • The school may share the educational records if the state has a juvenile justice exception that allows schools to release records to juvenile justice personnel in order to assist youth in the system prior to adjudication;
  • Any of the above

Scenario 2

Juvenile Probation, Prosecutor's Office and School

START

Fifteen year-old James was taken into custody by a School Resource Officer (SRO) for failing to follow school rules or policies and being "ungovernable," defined in the state's children's code as a violation of law.

NEXT

The SRO brought James to a Multi-Agency Resource Center where the staff completed an intake form, which did not include any information about James' school attendance or James's past school disciplinary history.

NEXT

James gets a court date to contest his governability, before which the prosecuting attorney's office and the probation office both contact the school.

NEXT

The prosecuting attorney's office and the probation office want James's school attendance record, past school disciplinary history, and information about services and interventions the school has offered James in the past.

NEXT

It is not clear from the scenario what the prosecuting attorney and probation office want to do with the information. These offices may use the information obtained to prosecute James for being ungovernable as alleged in the petition. The offices may use the information to divert James from adjudication and into some type of alternative program.

What does the law permit the school to do with the information?

  • C. The laws permit the school to share the educational records if the state has a juvenile justice exception that allows schools to release records to juvenile justice personnel in order to assist youth in the system prior to adjudication;

NEXT

Partially correct. The school requires prior parental consent to release the educational record to the prosecutor and the probation office. But B and C also apply.

Partially correct. The alternative to obtaining parental consent is for the prosecutor/probation office to serve the school with a court order or subpoena. Upon receipt, the school must make a "reasonable effort" to notify the student's parent of the order or subpoena before complying. But A and C also apply.

Partially correct, but A and B also apply. One exception to FERPA that may apply here is the state law juvenile justice exception. Under this exception, individual states may enact statutes that permit schools to release records to juvenile justice personnel in order to assist youth in the system prior to adjudication if the recipient of the records certifies in writing that he or she will not disclose the information to a third party without first obtaining parental consent. If such a law exists in this state, the school may release the education records to the prosecutor/probation offices.
